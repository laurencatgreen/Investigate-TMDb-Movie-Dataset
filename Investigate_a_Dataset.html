Introduction¶

This dataset is taken from The Movie Database (TMDb) and contains data collected for just over 10,000 movies from the years 1960 to 2015. The dataset contains information about each movie such as the genres it falls under, release date, user ratings, cast, production companies, budget, revenue and so on.

NumPy arrays and Pandas Series and DataFrames will be used in order address the following questions in this study.

Questions posed:¶

Q1: Which genres have the highest ratings on average?¶

Q2: What are the top 10 production companies that have made the most money per year?¶

Q3: What is the correlation between movie budget and average vote (do movies with a higher budget receive better ratings)?¶

Step 3:¶

Instead of using the mean vote_average for each genre type, a more accurate comparison would be to use the weighted average for each genre type thus taking the vote count into account. The weighted average is similar to the arithmetic mean (average), except each data point does not contribute equally to the final average value. Instead, some data points contribute more to the final average than others as each data point has a weight associated with each point and these weightings determine the relative importance of each data point on the overall average.

The weighted average is defined as:

$$weighted\: average =\frac{\sum_{i=1}^n(v_i*w_i)}{\sum_{i=1}^n w_i}$$

$$where: v_i = vote\ average, w_i = vote\ count$$

In [18]:
# Groupby genres, taking the weighted mean for vote_average for each genre type
# The numpy function applies the weighted average definition above
weighted_averages_result = result_df.groupby('genres').apply(lambda x: np.average(x['vote_average'], weights=x['vote_count']))

# Convert Series to DataFrame and use reset_index to make the genres index a column
weighted_averages_result_df = pd.DataFrame(weighted_averages_result, columns=['weighted_vote_average']).reset_index()

# Sort vote_average from highest to lowest vote
weighted_averages_result_df = weighted_averages_result_df.sort_values('weighted_vote_average', ascending=False)

# Calculate the correlation coefficient using NumPy's built in function
# indexing [0][1] gives the correlation of x,y
r = np.corrcoef(movies_df['budget_adj'], movies_df['vote_average'])[0][1]

# print Pearson's r
print(r)

0.0930389817834

The correlation coefficient is an indication of the strength of a linear relationship between two variables. A correlation coefficient equal to 1 indicates a perfect fit, thus the closer the correlation coefficient to 1, the better the fit. The correlation coefficient was calculated to be 0.093, this indicates a very poor fit of the data, indicating that there is no clear correlation of movie budget with average vote ratings.

Conclusions¶

Q1: Which movie genres have the highest ratings on average?¶

In this question, we investigated which movie genres have the highest ratings on average.

This was done in two ways:

  1. Using the mean function to find the mean vote rating for each genre
  2. Using NumPy's weighted average function to find the weighted average vote rating for each genre

Q2: What are the top 10 production companies that have made the most money per year?¶

The results for this question are depicted graphically in Fig 5. Here we see that LucasFilm made the most money in a year period with a whopping 2.75e+09 Dollars (in terms of Dollars 2010) in 1977, this was the year of the release of Star Wars: Episode IV – A New Hope.

Q3: What is the correlation between movie budget and average vote (do movies with a higher budget receive better ratings)?¶

In this question it was discovered that a lot of the Budget data was found to be zero or virtually zero. Thus rows that were less than one million dollars were excluded from the DataFrame. The correlation coefficient (Pearson's r) was calculated and found to be 0.093 which indicates a very poor fit. Thus there is no clear correlation of movie budget with average vote ratings.

Limitations¶

Although this dataset looked very itriguing to begin with, most of the data provided was categorical nominal data with some quantitative data. Much of the categorical data overlapped with other movies making it difficult to infer that any one factor was better than another. For example, Science Fiction was not among the most popular genre types, however, LucasFilm released Star Wars Episode IV – A New Hope in 1977, the year in which LucasFilm made the most income out of any other production company in the dataset.

Multiple variables (some of which cannot be quantified easily) affect a movies user rating and net income (these are not the only variables which quantify a movies success but are probably the most important), this may differ for every movie and comparing data with one another does not yield much useful insight into determining whether or not a movie will be successful. Production company, cast and the director (among many other factors) do play an important role in whether or not a movie is succesful, a more in depth study needs to be done in order to determine which factors make a movie successful, however, the answer is still not cut and dry.
